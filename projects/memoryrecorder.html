Memory Recorder

Project Overview

The Memory Recorder is my long-term research initiative, aimed at developing tools to capture and reconstruct aspects of human experience by combining multimodal biological signals with computational models. Inspired by the intersection of chemistry, electrical activity, and cognition, my vision is to create a framework that links memory processes with measurable brain dynamics. My current work—benchmarking emotion prediction from EEG and developing data analysis pipelines—represents early steps toward this direction.

I recognize that remembering is not a literal replay of experience but a fallible reconstruction. Hence, I believe that we do not need to capture and replay every molecular event to stimulate a "replay". With this in mind, I aim to integrate emotional and sensory signals (visual, auditory, and olfactory etc.) to better map memory states. Because emotions are complex and difficult to capture through a single modality, I am interested in integrating EEG, ECG, and EOG with biochemical measures such as neurotransmitters and hormones. Here I believe my biochemistry background offers a distinctive edge, allowing me to bridge chemical and electrical perspectives in neuroscience.

Psychedelic compounds may provide an important model in this pursuit, as they can induce reproducible alterations in brain activity and conscious experience. Studying such states through a multimodal framework could help reveal fundamental principles of how memories and emotions are encoded, reconstructed, and modulated.

If realized, such a framework could have far-reaching applications — from advancing memory consolidation and informing interventions for conditions such as Alzheimer’s disease and PTSD, to addressing the longstanding philosophical question of identity. Since memory is arguably the most defining aspect of who we are, progress in this area would represent not only a scientific milestone but also a deeper contribution to our understanding of the human self.
